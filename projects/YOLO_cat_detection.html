Cat detection with AI model

I have trained a YOLO (You Only Look Once) model on Google Colaboratory to detect cats in images. The YOLO algorithm is a popular object detection method that can accurately identify objects within an image while also providing their bounding box coordinates. For more information, read full case study below.

GitHub Link

Project Overview

The YOLO algorithm is a popular object detection method that can accurately identify objects within an image while also providing their bounding box coordinates. The process of training an AI, such as a YOLO model, involves several steps. First, a dataset of labeled images is collected and preprocessed. Next, the model is initialized and trained on the dataset using a training algorithm, such as stochastic gradient descent. During the training process, the model's parameters are adjusted to minimize the error between the predicted and actual output. Finally, the model is tested on a separate dataset to evaluate its performance.

However, training an AI can also come with its own set of challenges. One of the most common problems is overfitting, which occurs when the model becomes too complex and performs well on the training dataset but poorly on unseen data. Another issue is underfitting, which occurs when the model is too simple and is unable to accurately fit the training data. Additionally, the quality and size of the dataset can also greatly impact the performance of the model.

Case Study:

In my training of the YOLO model, I used the Darknet framework, which is an open-source neural network framework written in C and CUDA. To stich it all together, I used Google Colaboratory, which is a free cloud service that allows you to write and execute Python in your browser. It has powerful hardware and is a great tool for training AI models (even with the free version). I've opted to use the free version of Google Colaboratory, which provides 12 hours of GPU runtime per day. I've manually downloaded over 390 sample photos of cats in various poses and backgrounds. Loaded them up into LabelImg, which is a graphical image annotation tool, and labeled them as "cat" class.

After that, I've split the dataset into 80% training and 20% validation. Loaded them up into Google Drive and mounted it to Google Colaboratory. The first model was trained for 2000 epochs, which took about 4 hours. The results were not very good, so I've decided to train the model for 5000 epochs, which took about 10 hours. The results were much better, but still not perfect. Sometimes some innacurate detections were made. For example, the model sometimes detected a cat in a picture of a dog or another animal. Overall the results were pretty good - regarding this is my first time training a model. I've learned a lot about the process of training an AI model and how to avoid some common pitfalls, it was not as simple as it seemed. I'd recommend anyone who is interested in AI to try training their own model and see how it comes out.
